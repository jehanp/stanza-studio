# The Potter's Wheel — Imagery Notes

**Scripture:** Jeremiah 18:1-6
**Key Verse:** _"So I went down to the potter's house, and there he was working at his wheel. And the vessel he was making of clay was spoiled in the potter's hand, and he reworked it into another vessel, as it seemed good to the potter to do."_

---

## Core Theme

The right of the maker to reshape what went wrong — deconstruction as the prelude to a better design, not as punishment.

## Lyric Angle

God sends Jeremiah to watch a potter at work. The potter begins a vessel, but it goes wrong on the wheel — spoiled in his hand. Instead of discarding the clay, he crushes it down and starts again. A different vessel. Still the same clay. The lyric sits inside the experience of being the clay — feeling yourself collapse, losing the shape you thought was your identity, wondering if this is abandonment or artistry. The theological edge: "Can the clay say to the potter, what are you making?" The answer is implied: no. But the comfort is in the hands — they're not punishing, they're remaking. The vessel that went wrong is not thrown away. It's pressed down and re-thrown into something the potter considers good.
